FURNITURE RESTORATION GUIDE - FINISHING TECHNIQUES
Source: Master Craftsman Workshop Manual
Last Updated: January 2026
Category: Finishing
Priority: Medium

=== STRIPPING & PREPARATION ===

When to Strip vs. Restore Existing Finish:
STRIP if:
- Finish is heavily damaged (alligatoring, deep cracks)
- Multiple incompatible finish layers present
- Changing color or type of finish
- Paint removal needed

RESTORE if:
- Original finish is intact but dull
- Minor scratches or wear only
- Historical value in preserving original finish
- Shellac or wax finish (can be revived)

Chemical Stripping Method:
Material: All wood types
Safety: CRITICAL - Use outdoors or well-ventilated area, wear gloves and eye protection

1. Apply chemical stripper with natural bristle brush
2. Wait recommended time (15-30 minutes)
3. Remove softened finish with plastic scraper
4. Work with grain direction
5. Use steel wool (0000) for detailed areas
6. Neutralize with mineral spirits or water (check product instructions)
7. Allow 24-48 hours drying time before refinishing

Heat Gun Method:
Material: Painted wood (NOT veneer)
Caution: Risk of scorching wood or fire

1. Hold heat gun 6-8 inches from surface
2. Move constantly to avoid burning
3. Scrape softened finish immediately
4. Never use on veneer (will lift glue)

Sanding:
- Start with 80-grit for rough surfaces
- Progress through 120, 150, 220 grit
- Always sand with the grain
- Vacuum between grit changes
- Final pass with 320 for glass-smooth surface

=== STAINING ===

Material: Bare wood
Application: Post-stripping, pre-finishing

Types of Stain:

Oil-Based Stain:
- Longer working time
- Deeper penetration
- Best for: Oak, ash, open-grain woods
- Dry time: 6-8 hours

Water-Based Stain:
- Quick drying
- Raises grain (requires extra sanding)
- Best for: Even color, low odor projects
- Dry time: 2-3 hours

Gel Stain:
- Thick consistency, less dripping
- Best for: Vertical surfaces, blotch-prone woods (pine, maple)
- Even color distribution
- Dry time: 8-12 hours

Application Technique:
1. Test on scrap or hidden area first
2. Apply with brush or cloth in direction of grain
3. Allow penetration (5-15 minutes depending on desired darkness)
4. Wipe excess with clean cloth
5. Let dry completely before topcoat
6. Apply second coat for darker color if needed

Blotch Prevention (Pine, Maple, Cherry):
1. Apply wood conditioner before stain
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Lightly sand with 320-grit
4. Apply stain while conditioner still slightly damp

=== TOP COAT FINISHES ===

SHELLAC (Traditional French Polish):
Material: All woods, especially for antique restoration
Difficulty: High skill required

Process:
1. Mix shellac flakes with denatured alcohol (2 lb cut for filling, 1 lb for top coats)
2. Apply thin coats with brush or padding technique
3. Sand lightly with 400-grit between coats
4. Build 3-5 coats for protection
5. Final coat: 1/2 lb cut, padded for glass finish
6. Dry time: 2-3 hours between coats

Advantages: Repairable, beautiful warm tone, traditional
Disadvantages: Not water-resistant, alcohol sensitive

OIL-BASED POLYURETHANE:
Material: High-traffic surfaces, tabletops
Difficulty: Easy
Sheen: Gloss, semi-gloss, satin

Process:
1. Thin first coat 10% with mineral spirits (better penetration)
2. Apply with high-quality natural bristle brush
3. Flow on, don't over-brush
4. Dry 24 hours
5. Sand with 320-grit
6. Apply 2-3 additional coats
7. Wait 72 hours for full cure

Advantages: Very durable, water-resistant
Disadvantages: Yellows over time, difficult to repair

WATER-BASED POLYURETHANE:
Material: Light woods, modern projects
Difficulty: Easy
Sheen: Clear, non-yellowing

Process:
1. Do not thin
2. Apply with synthetic bristle brush
3. Use long, even strokes
4. Dry time: 2-3 hours
5. Light sand with 320-grit between coats
6. Apply 3 coats minimum
7. Cure time: 48 hours

Advantages: Clear, no yellowing, low odor, quick dry
Disadvantages: Less durable than oil-based, raises grain slightly

LACQUER (Spray Application):
Material: Professional projects, production furniture
Difficulty: High (requires spray equipment)

Process:
1. Seal with sanding sealer
2. Sand with 320-grit
3. Apply 3-4 thin coats (better than 1 heavy coat)
4. Dry time: 30 minutes between coats
5. Wet sand with 600-grit after final coat (optional)
6. Rub out with rubbing compound for glass finish

Advantages: Fast drying, beautiful finish, repairable
Disadvantages: Requires spray equipment, strong fumes, ventilation needed

TUNG OIL (Hand-Rubbed Finish):
Material: Walnut, teak, open-grain woods
Difficulty: Easy, time-consuming

Process:
1. Apply generous coat with cloth
2. Let penetrate 10-15 minutes
3. Wipe all excess completely
4. Dry 24 hours minimum
5. Repeat 4-6 times
6. Final coats: very thin application
7. Buff with soft cloth

Advantages: Natural look, easy repair, highlights grain
Disadvantages: Multiple coats needed, minimal protection, long cure time

=== RUBBING OUT & POLISHING ===

For Professional Glass Finish:
Material: Fully cured finish (polyurethane, lacquer)
Difficulty: Medium

Process:
1. Wet sand with 600-grit paper (use water or oil as lubricant)
2. Progress to 800, 1000, 1200 grit
3. Apply rubbing compound with soft cloth in circular motion
4. Follow with polishing compound
5. Final buff with clean microfiber cloth
6. Apply paste wax (optional)

Result: Mirror-like, glass-smooth finish

=== WAX APPLICATION (Final Protection) ===

Material: Over sealed finish (not bare wood)
Purpose: Protection, enhanced luster, maintenance

Types:
- Paste Wax: Traditional, best protection
- Liquid Wax: Easier application, less durable

Application:
1. Ensure finish is fully cured
2. Apply thin layer with soft cloth
3. Let haze (5-10 minutes)
4. Buff with clean cloth in circular motion
5. Reapply every 3-6 months

Best Practice: Apply 2-3 thin coats rather than 1 heavy coat
