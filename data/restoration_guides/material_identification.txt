FURNITURE RESTORATION GUIDE - MATERIAL IDENTIFICATION
Source: Historical Furniture Conservation Institute
Last Updated: February 2026
Category: Material Guide
Priority: High

=== WOOD TYPES ===

SOLID WOOD vs VENEER
Visual Test:
- Solid wood: grain patterns continue around all edges
- Veneer: thin layer shows edge banding or substrate

Weight Test:
- Solid hardwood: significantly heavier
- Veneer over particle board/MDF: lighter

Common Vintage Woods:

OAK (Red & White)
Characteristics: Prominent grain, open pores, heavy
Color: Light tan to reddish-brown
Era: 1880s-1940s (Golden Oak era)
Grain Pattern: Pronounced rays and cathedral patterns
Best for: Structural pieces, Mission style furniture

WALNUT
Characteristics: Straight grain, fine texture, rich color
Color: Dark chocolate to purplish-brown
Era: 1700s-1850s (Colonial), 1960s-70s (Mid-Century)
Grain Pattern: Generally straight with occasional swirl
Best for: High-end furniture, veneers

MAHOGANY
Characteristics: Medium to coarse texture, reddish color
Color: Reddish-brown to deep red
Era: 1750-1850 (Federal period), 1920s-30s
Grain Pattern: Interlocked or straight, ribbon stripe
Best for: Fine furniture, carved details

MAPLE (Hard Maple)
Characteristics: Fine, even texture, very hard
Color: Creamy white to light reddish-brown
Era: 1700s-1800s (Colonial), 1950s-60s (Modern)
Grain Pattern: Generally straight, sometimes curly or bird's eye
Best for: Chairs, tabletops, turned legs

PINE (White, Yellow, Heart)
Characteristics: Soft, lightweight, prominent growth rings
Color: Pale yellow to reddish-brown
Era: 1700s-1900s (Country furniture)
Grain Pattern: Straight with visible growth rings
Best for: Rustic furniture, painted pieces

CHERRY
Characteristics: Fine, straight grain, ages beautifully
Color: Light pinkish-brown, darkens with age
Color: Reddish-brown when aged
Era: 1750-1850 (American Federal), Modern reproductions
Grain Pattern: Fine, closed grain, straight
Best for: Fine furniture, premium pieces

=== FINISH TYPES ===

SHELLAC
Appearance: Warm, amber glow
Test: Dissolves with denatured alcohol
Era: Pre-1920s common, still used today
Repair: Re-amalgamate with alcohol, French polish

LACQUER
Appearance: Clear, hard, glossy
Test: Dissolves with lacquer thinner
Era: 1920s onward
Repair: Must strip and refinish, cannot touch up easily

VARNISH (Oil-based)
Appearance: Thick, durable, yellows with age
Test: Resistant to alcohol, softens with paint stripper
Era: 1850s onward
Repair: Sand and recoat, or strip completely

POLYURETHANE
Appearance: Plastic-like, very durable
Test: Resistant to most solvents
Era: 1960s onward
Repair: Sand between coats, requires complete removal for refinishing

OIL FINISH (Tung, Linseed, Danish)
Appearance: Low sheen, natural look
Test: Penetrates wood, not surface film
Era: Traditional and modern
Repair: Clean and reapply additional coats

WAX
Appearance: Low luster, soft feel
Test: Soft, easily scratched, melts with heat
Era: Traditional maintenance finish
Repair: Clean and reapply

=== HARDWARE & METALS ===

BRASS
Characteristics: Yellow-gold color, tarnishes green
Era: All periods
Identification: Non-magnetic, heavier than steel
Cleaning: Brass polish, vinegar and salt paste

BRONZE
Characteristics: Reddish-brown, develops patina
Era: High-end antiques
Identification: Very heavy, non-magnetic
Cleaning: Gentle soap, preserve patina

IRON (Cast, Wrought)
Characteristics: Black or rusty, magnetic
Era: 1800s-early 1900s
Identification: Magnetic, rusts
Treatment: Remove rust, apply protective coating

STEEL (Nickel-plated)
Characteristics: Bright silver, magnetic
Era: 1920s-1940s
Identification: Magnetic, chrome-like appearance
Restoration: Re-plate if worn, or polish

=== DAMAGE IDENTIFICATION ===

OXIDATION
Appearance: Darkening, black spots, dull finish
Cause: Exposure to air and moisture
Material: All woods and finishes
Solution: Clean with appropriate solvent, refinish if severe

CHECKING (Alligatoring)
Appearance: Network of fine cracks in finish
Cause: Incompatible finish layers, age, temperature changes
Material: Lacquer, varnish
Solution: Strip and refinish

CLOUDINESS
Appearance: Milky, hazy finish
Cause: Moisture trapped in finish
Material: Lacquer, shellac
Solution: Re-amalgamate or buff with compounds

WOODWORM/BEETLE DAMAGE
Appearance: Small round holes, powder residue
Cause: Wood-boring insects
Material: Solid wood
Solution: Treat with borate solution, fill holes if active infestation stopped
